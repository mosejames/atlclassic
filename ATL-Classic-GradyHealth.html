The Early Days (1950s-1960s)

Golden era of HBCU football with legendary coaches Jake Gaither (FAMU) and John Merritt (TSU). Oct 25, 1969: TSU 33, FAMU 20 with 20,000 fans at TSU.

Atlanta Football Classic Era (1989-2010)

1989: Inaugural Classic at Bobby Dodd Stadium. FAMU 21, TSU 9. Moved to Georgia Dome in 1992.

2003: Record 70,185 attendance — largest HBCU classic crowd ever.

This rivalry packed the Georgia Dome year after year — averaging 60,212 fans per game over a decade. No matter who was winning, the fans showed up.

FAMU won 8 straight (2002-2009) before TSU answered with 6 consecutive. The back-and-forth kept crowds at capacity and earned national broadcast attention on ESPN and NBCSN.

Post-Classic Era (2011-2017)

2015: The Atlanta Football Classic era ends. The annual HBCU showcase in Atlanta continues as the Celebration Bowl.

2017: Last meeting in Tampa — TSU 24, FAMU 13. Nine-year gap begins.

The Return (2026)

September 19, 2026 — The ATL Classic at Mercedes-Benz Stadium.

First meeting in 9 years. First of a two-game home-and-home series. The 56th all-time meeting. The rivalry returns home.

A Partnership Built For You

Why Grady Health
Belongs Here

Grady Health System has served Atlanta for over 130 years. It is the largest public hospital in Georgia, one of the busiest Level I Trauma Centers in the nation, and the closest major medical facility to Mercedes-Benz Stadium. If something goes wrong at the ATL Classic, Grady is already the hospital on call. This partnership means Grady is also the hospital helping the community stay well.

But the deeper story is the HBCU connection. Grady is the primary teaching hospital for Morehouse School of Medicine, one of only four historically Black medical schools in America. MSM was founded in 1975 specifically to train physicians who would serve underserved communities, and Grady has been its clinical home since day one. MSM students complete their rotations at Grady. MSM faculty serve as attending physicians. The two institutions are structurally inseparable. And MSM produces more Black male primary care physicians than any other medical school in the country.

Grady sits adjacent to the Atlanta University Center, home to Morehouse College, Spelman College, Clark Atlanta University, and Morris Brown College. It is, geographically and institutionally, the hospital of the HBCU community. Its patient population is approximately 70-80% Black/African American. Multiple generations of Black Atlantans have a Grady story. It is one of the few institutions that cuts across class lines, serving everyone from families without insurance to corporate executives in a crisis.

The ATL Classic audience is not a demographic Grady needs to reach. It is the community Grady already serves. Every person in that stadium either has been to Grady, knows someone who has, or will at some point. This partnership does not require Grady to build awareness with a new audience. It requires Grady to show up, visibly and proudly, for the people who already know its name.

A Community Health Partner designation at the ATL Classic gives Grady a platform to do what it already does, deliver preventive care, health education, and community outreach, at a scale and visibility that no hospital marketing budget can replicate. 55,000+ fans, national broadcast coverage, and a community health activation that generates real outcomes: screenings completed, referrals made, lives potentially saved.

This is not a stretch for Grady. This is Grady's mission, amplified. Atlanta's hospital, built on an HBCU medical school partnership, showing up for the HBCU community on the biggest stage in southern football.
